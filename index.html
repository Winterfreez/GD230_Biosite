CONCEPT

Photorealism is a style of art that strives to reproduce a detailed copy of a photograph using human hands. The resulting works of art can very easily be mistaken as photographs themselves due to the incredibly high level of accuracy and detail. The artists take great care to eliminate or mask any telltale signs of hand-crafted work such as brush strokes.

METHOD

The artist would take several photographs of the subject they wished to portray and select the best one. Next, they would use a projector or similar method of transferring the actual image onto the medium they wished to create their work on. The artist would then begin replicating the projection using manual methods with painstaking attention to detail. The actual medium of reproduction varied from paints (both oil and acrylic) to airbrushing. Even those that chose to work with paint often used airbrushes to touch up the final work, which was also a common practice for touching up actual photographs.

ORIGINS

Photorealism began in 1950's America at the hands of Richard Estes. Estes used photographs extensively as reference material like many artists did at the time, but by the 1960's he began trying to replicate the photographs themselves as accurately as possible. His work, primarily landscapes, were startlingly life-like which caught on with other artists such as Ralph Goings and Audrey Flack. Unlike many other movements, the notable artists practicing Photorealism work independently of one another with little contact.

SUBJECT MATTER

Most Photorealistic works feature classical subjects such as still life arrangements, landscapes, and the occasional portrait. The artists typically selected neutral subjects, trying to also capture the culturally agnostic lens of the camera they sought to emulate. Despite the strive for neutralism, most of the ordinary subjects (such as fast food establishments and diners) gave the works of art a clearly American feeling.

INDIVIDUAL STYLES

The taste of the artists were quite varied, probably in large part due to the independent nature of it's practitioners. Flack was very well known for her work with still life settings, usually consisting of everyday objects like playing cards, mirrors, makeup, and fruit. The resulting images were usually shockingly colorful and opulent. Goings was fascinated by fast food scenes, his work typically featuring ketchup bottles and salt shakers gleaming in bright light. Estes, the one to start it all, preferred city streets and urban landscapes. Ironically, his work was replicated with a magnificent level of accuracy, but was devoid of many features of said locations such as trash or even people.

EVOLUTION

As far as artistic movements go, Photorealism was a rather small affair. At its height, it boasted only a handful of practitioners and never reached wide acclaim by the rest of the art world. Most other artists saw the use of projectors and photographs as cheating while others considered it an assault on the advances made by the subjective material of abstract artists. It eventually evolved into a new movement known as Hyper-realism which focuses on rendering the same photographic quality images from the imagination instead of actual photos
